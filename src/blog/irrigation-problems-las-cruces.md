If you've got an irrigation system in Las Cruces, something is going to break eventually. It's not a question of if — it's a question of what gets to it first: the hard water, the UV, the wind, or the monsoons.

The good news is that most common irrigation problems are diagnosable, and a lot of them are fixable without calling anyone. The ones that aren't? You'll save money by recognizing them early instead of letting a small leak turn into a big water bill.

Here's what we see most often — and what you can do about each one.

## Hard Water and Clogged Emitters

This is the number one irrigation problem in Las Cruces, and most people don't realize it until plants start dying.

Las Cruces water is hard — roughly 140 parts per million (about 8 grains per gallon) of dissolved calcium and magnesium. That's classified as "hard" on the national scale, and it's enough to cause real problems in [drip irrigation systems](/services/irrigation/drip-irrigation/). Those tiny emitter openings — some as small as a pencil lead — gradually crust over with white mineral deposits. Flow drops, plants get less water than they need, and the system looks like it's running fine from the valve box.

**The DIY fix:**

Flush your drip system at least twice a year — once before the summer watering season starts and once after monsoons. Open the end caps on each lateral line and let water run through for 2–3 minutes to push out sediment and loose scale.

For emitters that are already clogged, pull them off and soak in white vinegar for 15–30 minutes. The acid dissolves the calcium buildup. A toothpick or pin clears anything left in the opening. If the emitter still doesn't flow right after cleaning, replace it — they cost less than a dollar each at any hardware store.

**What about inline filters?** It depends on your water source. If you're on a private well — and plenty of homes in the Mesilla Valley, Doña Ana, and east mesa are — a mesh filter at each valve is worth it. Well water carries sand, sediment, and organic debris that will plug emitters fast. Clean those filters every few months and they'll pay for themselves.

But if you're on city water (LCU, Moongate, DAMDWCA), inline filters are mostly a waste. Municipal water is already filtered, and the calcium that actually clogs your emitters is *dissolved* — a mesh screen can't catch dissolved minerals. All you're doing is adding a pressure drop and another thing that needs cleaning. Put that money toward pressure-compensating emitters with wider flow paths instead — they're self-flushing and far less prone to clogging in the first place.

## Broken or Stuck Spray Heads

If you mow your own lawn, you've probably hit a [sprinkler head](/services/irrigation/sprinkler-systems/) at least once. It happens. Lawn equipment is the number one killer of pop-up spray heads in Las Cruces — followed by UV brittleness. After a few years of desert sun, the plastic gets fragile and cracks from normal foot traffic or even temperature swings.

**Signs to watch for:** a head that won't pop up, one that pops up but sprays sideways, water bubbling up around the base, or a zone that has a noticeably dry spot.

**The DIY fix:**

Replacing a pop-up spray head is one of the easiest irrigation repairs there is:

1. Turn off the water to that zone
2. Dig carefully around the head — about 4 inches deep and 6 inches wide
3. Unscrew the old head counterclockwise from the riser (the vertical pipe fitting)
4. Wrap the riser threads with plumber's tape (Teflon tape)
5. Screw on the new head hand-tight, plus a quarter turn
6. Turn the zone on and check for leaks before backfilling

The whole thing takes 10–15 minutes and costs $3–8 for the replacement head. Match the brand and model if you can — mixing head types on the same zone causes uneven coverage because different heads have different flow rates and throw distances.

**Pro tip:** While you're at it, check whether the head height is correct. Spray heads should sit flush with the ground when retracted. If they've settled below grade, dirt washes in and jams the riser. If they're sticking up above grade, that's why they keep getting hit.

## Controller Programming Mistakes

This one isn't a broken part — it's a settings problem. And it wastes more water (and money) than most physical leaks.

The most common controller mistakes we see in Las Cruces:

**Multiple start times running.** Most controllers have Programs A, B, and C. Many homeowners accidentally set start times on all three, which means the system runs its entire cycle two or three times. If your lawn looks overwatered and your bill is high, check this first.

**AM/PM mixup.** Your sprinklers are supposed to run at 5:00 AM, not 5:00 PM. Running during the restricted window (10 AM–6 PM, April through September) violates [Las Cruces watering restrictions](/blog/summer-survival-guide-las-cruces-landscape/#the-watering-schedule-you-have-to-follow) — and wastes water to evaporation. The city's violation hotline is 575-528-4444.

**No seasonal adjustment.** Your June schedule shouldn't be your March schedule. Desert plants need less water in spring and fall. If you haven't touched your controller since the installer set it up, you're probably overwatering half the year and underwatering the other half.

**Dead backup battery.** When the power flickers (monsoon season, anyone?), your controller resets to factory defaults. A fresh 9-volt battery keeps your programming intact during outages.

**The DIY fix:** Read your controller manual. If you lost it, the model number is on the face plate — every major brand (Rain Bird, Hunter, Orbit) has free manuals online. Set one program with the correct start time, dial in your zone run times, and set your watering days to match your address (even addresses: Tue/Thu/Sat, odd: Wed/Fri/Sun).

Or consider upgrading to a [smart controller](/services/irrigation/smart-controllers/). They auto-adjust watering based on weather data, respect the city's restricted hours automatically, and cut back after rain so you're not watering a soaked yard. The water savings typically pay for the controller within a year or two.

## Mystery Water Bill: The Leak Test

If your water bill jumped and nothing else changed, you probably have a leak somewhere in your irrigation system.

Here's the thing people forget: most irrigation leaks only waste water while that zone is actually running. If you're watering 3 days a week for an hour per zone, a leaking zone runs about 12 hours a month. A 1-gallon-per-minute leak during those 12 hours wastes around 720 gallons — not catastrophic, but that's real money at [DAMDWCA rates](/blog/xeriscaping-water-savings-las-cruces/#the-rate-breakdown) ($5.22–$6.99 per 1,000 gallons), and it adds up over a season. The bigger cost is usually the plants that aren't getting enough water because pressure is dropping at the leak point instead of reaching the rest of the zone.

**The DIY leak test:**

1. Turn off everything in the house that uses water
2. Find your water meter (usually near the street in a ground box)
3. Check the flow indicator — it's a small triangle or dial on the meter face. If it's moving with everything shut off, water is going somewhere it shouldn't
4. Walk your property and look for: soggy spots that never dry out, areas of grass that are noticeably greener than the rest, water pooling near valve boxes, and hissing sounds near pipes

**Common leak locations:** cracked pipes from soil shifting (Las Cruces caliche is notorious for this), loose fittings at valve connections, worn valve diaphragms that let water seep through, and cracked heads or broken risers.

Simple leaks at heads and fittings are DIY-able. Underground pipe leaks and valve diaphragm replacements are where most homeowners should call in a pro — digging in the wrong spot or improperly seating a diaphragm usually makes things worse.

## Sun-Damaged Drip Lines

Las Cruces gets over 300 days of sunshine a year. That UV exposure is brutal on plastic, and drip irrigation tubing is no exception.

Drip lines that sit exposed on top of bare ground — not buried under rock or mulch — deteriorate fast. The tubing gets brittle, hairline cracks develop, and connections pop loose. You'll see water spraying where it shouldn't or wet spots between emitters instead of at them.

**The DIY fix:** Replace the damaged sections. Quarter-inch drip tubing and barbed connectors are cheap and available at any irrigation supply store. Cut out the cracked section, use a barbed coupling to splice in new tubing, and make sure the connection is tight.

**Prevent it:** Bury drip lines 2–3 inches under decorative rock or mulch. This blocks UV, keeps the tubing cooler (which extends its life), and protects it from foot traffic and wind shifting. If your system was installed with exposed tubing, it's worth the afternoon to cover it properly — you'll stop replacing cracked lines every year or two.

## Wind and Coverage Problems

If you have spray heads, you already know this one. Las Cruces wind is relentless, and it wreaks havoc on sprinkler coverage. A spray pattern designed for calm conditions gets blown sideways, soaking the sidewalk while half your lawn stays dry.

**The DIY fix:**

Adjust your spray arc and radius. Most pop-up heads have a small screw on top that controls the throw distance — turn it clockwise to reduce the radius. The spray arc is adjustable on most heads too (check your model).

If wind is a consistent problem on certain zones, consider converting those zones from spray heads to rotary nozzles (like MP Rotators). Rotary nozzles throw heavier streams at a lower angle, which resists wind drift much better than fine mist from standard spray heads. They also apply water more slowly, which means less runoff on compacted or sloped ground.

For plant beds and trees, the answer is usually [switching to drip irrigation](/blog/drip-vs-sprinklers-las-cruces/) entirely. Drip delivers water directly to the root zone at ground level — wind can't touch it.

## Monsoon Damage

The Southwest monsoon runs mid-June through September, and Las Cruces gets over half its annual rainfall — about 4.9 inches — in just those few months. (For a full prep checklist beyond just irrigation, see our [monsoon season prep guide](/blog/monsoon-prep-las-cruces/).) The problem isn't the total amount; it's the delivery. It arrives as intense, short bursts that can dump an inch in 30 minutes.

That kind of runoff moves things. Drip lines get buried under silt, emitters get disconnected or packed with mud, and valve boxes fill with water. If you're on a slope, [erosion](/services/irrigation/erosion-control/) can expose buried pipes.

**After every big storm:** Walk your zones. Turn on each zone one at a time and verify that emitters are still flowing, heads are still spraying correctly, and no new wet spots have appeared where they shouldn't. This takes 20 minutes and can save you from discovering a problem in August when your plants are already stressed.

Check valve boxes too — they should be dry inside. Standing water in a valve box means either the box drainage is blocked or a fitting is leaking.

And after a good monsoon rain, turn off your irrigation for 2–3 days. The ground is saturated. Running your system on top of natural rainfall wastes water and can promote root rot, especially in desert-adapted plants. A [smart controller](/services/irrigation/smart-controllers/) does this automatically with a rain sensor or weather data integration.

## When to Call a Pro

Some irrigation repairs are genuine DIY territory — replacing spray heads, cleaning filters, flushing drip lines, and reprogramming controllers. You don't need a contractor for that, and you'll save money doing it yourself.

But some problems will cost you more in wasted water and dead plants than a [professional repair](/services/irrigation/sprinkler-repair/) visit would:

**Call a pro for:**

- Underground pipe leaks — digging blind in caliche soil risks making it worse
- Valve replacement or rebuild — getting the diaphragm seated wrong causes more leaking
- Backflow preventer issues — this is a code and safety item
- Full system audits — if your coverage is uneven across the whole yard, individual head adjustments won't fix a design problem
- Zone conversions — switching spray zones to drip involves resizing pipe, adding pressure regulators, and recalculating flow rates

A [seasonal tune-up](/services/irrigation/seasonal-tune-ups/) twice a year — once before summer and once before winter — catches small problems before they turn into big bills. Think of it like an oil change for your irrigation system.
