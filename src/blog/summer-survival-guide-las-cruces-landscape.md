June through September is when Las Cruces earns its reputation. Daytime highs regularly push past 100°F, the wind pulls moisture out of everything, and your water bill starts climbing before you even notice. The sun is relentless, the soil bakes, and any plant that isn't adapted to this climate is working overtime just to stay alive.

But you don't have to lose your landscape to the heat. With the right strategy — watering smarter, protecting your soil, and knowing when to work *with* the desert instead of against it — your yard can make it through summer without looking like a war zone in October.

Here's what actually works.

## Water Deep, Not Often

This is the single biggest mistake homeowners make in Las Cruces summers: watering a little bit every single day.

It feels right — the heat is brutal, so more water should help, right? But frequent shallow watering trains plant roots to stay near the surface. And surface soil in southern New Mexico can hit 140°F on a July afternoon. Those shallow roots cook.

The better approach: water deeply and less frequently. Push moisture down 18–24 inches for shrubs and 24–36 inches for trees. For groundcovers and flower beds, aim for 8–12 inches. This encourages roots to grow down, where soil stays cooler and retains moisture longer.

**For drip irrigation systems:** Run longer cycles, fewer times per week. If your [drip system](/services/irrigation/drip-irrigation/) was running 30 minutes three times a week in May, try 45–60 minutes twice a week in July. You'll use less water overall and your plants will be healthier for it.

**For sprinkler systems:** This is harder to do efficiently with sprinklers because of evaporation loss. If you're still running [spray heads](/services/irrigation/sprinkler-systems/) on landscape beds, summer is a good time to consider converting those zones to drip. Sprinklers lose 30–50% of their output to evaporation and wind on a hot, breezy Las Cruces afternoon.

**For desert-adapted plants:** Once established (usually after their second summer), most xeric plants — agaves, desert willows, red yuccas, penstemon — need watering only every 10–14 days in the hottest months. Over-watering established desert plants is actually the fastest way to kill them. Root rot doesn't care how hot it is outside.

## The Watering Schedule You Have to Follow

Las Cruces Utilities enforces outdoor watering restrictions from April 1 through September 30:

- **Even-numbered addresses:** Tuesday, Thursday, Saturday
- **Odd-numbered addresses:** Wednesday, Friday, Sunday
- **No one waters on Monday**
- **No watering between 10 a.m. and 6 p.m.**

The morning window (before 10 a.m.) is your best bet anyway. Early morning watering loses less to evaporation, and plants have time to absorb moisture before the heat kicks in. Evening watering works in a pinch, but wet foliage overnight can invite fungal issues — something bermuda grass is already susceptible to in humid monsoon weeks.

If you have a [smart irrigation controller](/services/irrigation/smart-controllers/), program it to start cycles at 5–6 a.m. and finish before 10. Smart controllers can also adjust run times based on weather data, so they'll automatically cut back after a monsoon rain — saving water you'd otherwise waste.

**What about Doña Ana County residents outside city limits?** If you're on Moongate, DAMDWCA, or another mutual water system, you may not be subject to LCU's odd/even schedule — but the principles still apply. Water early, water deep, and watch your bill. DAMDWCA customers hit $5.22/1,000 gallons once they pass 10,000 gallons a month. Summer irrigation can push you into that tier fast.

## Don't Leave Soil Bare

Bare soil in Las Cruces summer is a problem. It bakes, it crusts over, and water runs off instead of soaking in. Any ground cover — rock, gravel, or organic mulch — is better than exposed dirt.

**Rock and gravel** are the standard here for good reason. They don't blow away in the wind, they don't break down in the UV, and they don't need replacing every year. A 2–3 inch layer of decorative rock keeps soil from crusting, reduces runoff, and gives your landscape a clean, finished look that fits the desert. It's what most Las Cruces properties use, and it works.

**Where organic mulch makes sense:** In protected planting beds — against a wall, inside a courtyard, around a tree well with edging — bark or pecan shell mulch can drop soil temperature by 10°F or more compared to rock. University of Arizona research confirmed this: organic material insulates rather than absorbing and re-radiating heat the way stone does. If you have specific plants that are heat-stressed (citrus, some ornamentals), a few inches of organic mulch in a sheltered bed can make the difference. But in open, wind-exposed areas, it'll blow away or decompose in one season. Save it for protected spots.

**Around trees:** Whether you're using rock or bark, keep material 3–4 inches away from the trunk. Anything piled against bark invites rot and pests — and in our climate, you won't notice the damage until the tree is already compromised.

## Get Ready for Monsoons

Here's the thing about Las Cruces summers that newcomers don't expect: we're not just hot and dry. We're hot and dry *until suddenly we're not.*

The Southwest monsoon runs June 15 through September 30, with the heaviest activity in July and August. Las Cruces gets only 9.1 inches of rain per year — but over half of it falls in just three months:

| Month | Avg. Rainfall | % of Days with Rain |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|
| January | 0.48" | 10.6% |
| February | 0.36" | 8.6% |
| March | 0.26" | 6.5% |
| April | 0.22" | 5.3% |
| May | 0.38" | 6.7% |
| June | 0.67" | 11.1% |
| **July** | **1.76"** | **28.7%** |
| **August** | **1.73"** | **27.1%** |
| **September** | **1.41"** | **17.4%** |
| October | 0.82" | 12.9% |
| November | 0.41" | 8.7% |
| December | 0.64" | 10.5% |

*Source: NOAA 1991–2020 averages, NMSU State University station*

July through September accounts for 4.9 inches — 54% of the year's total — almost all arriving as brief, intense storms. Your landscape goes from bone-dry to flash-flood conditions in the span of a single afternoon.

That means your landscape needs to handle two opposite extremes within the same season: drought stress *and* flash flooding.

**Before the first big storm:**

- **Check your grading.** Soil should slope away from your foundation at least 6 inches over the first 10 feet. If you've had settling or erosion, now is the time to fix it.
- **Clear drainage paths.** Gravel swales, [French drains](/services/irrigation/french-drains/), and any [drainage solutions](/services/irrigation/stormwater-mitigation/) on your property need to be clear of debris before July.
- **Inspect drip lines.** Monsoon runoff can bury, shift, or disconnect drip emitters. After the first heavy rain, walk your zones and check that water is still reaching your plants.
- **Secure loose rock and mulch.** In storm-prone beds or slopes, heavy rain can wash gravel and expose irrigation lines. Consider [erosion control](/services/irrigation/erosion-control/) measures on any slope steeper than about 3:1.

**After monsoon rains:** Turn off your irrigation for 2–3 days (or let your smart controller handle it). The ground is saturated. Watering on top of monsoon rain is the #1 cause of summer root rot in desert landscapes.

## If You Still Have a Lawn

Bermuda grass — the most common warm-season turf in Las Cruces — actually does well in heat. It thrives between 77–95°F and tolerates higher temperatures as long as it's getting adequate water. But there's a difference between surviving and thriving, and above 100°F, even bermuda is mostly just holding on.

**Summer lawn tips:**

- **Mow higher.** Raise your mowing height to 2–2.5 inches. Taller grass shades its own root zone and retains soil moisture better. Never remove more than one-third of the blade height in a single mow.
- **Leave the clippings.** They decompose quickly in this heat and return nitrogen to the soil. It's free fertilizer.
- **Water on your assigned days, early morning.** Bermuda needs about 1–1.5 inches of water per week in summer. With your three allowed watering days, that means putting down about half an inch per session.
- **Aerate if you haven't this year.** Late spring and summer are the right time for warm-season grasses. Compacted soil (especially our clay-heavy Mesilla Valley stuff) prevents water from reaching roots. [Aeration](/services/landscaping/aeration/) opens it up.
- **Hold off on heavy fertilization** until the worst heat passes. Pushing growth during extreme heat stresses the turf more. Light feeding is fine; heavy doses of nitrogen in July are asking for trouble.

## When the Desert Wins

Here's an honest observation from someone who installs both [artificial turf](/services/landscaping/artificial-turf/) and [natural sod](/services/landscaping/sod-installation/) in Las Cruces: if you're spending every summer fighting the climate, battling your water bill, adjusting sprinkler timers, and stressing about brown spots — it might be time to ask whether the fight is worth it.

[Xeriscaping](/services/landscaping/xeriscaping/) isn't giving up on your yard. It's redesigning it so it works *with* the climate instead of against it. A well-done xeric landscape still has color, texture, structure, and shade. It just doesn't need 5,000 gallons of water a month to look good.

And if you want the green-lawn look without the water bill or the maintenance headaches, [artificial turf](/services/landscaping/artificial-turf/) is another option — zero watering, no mowing, no odd/even schedule. Yes, it gets hot in direct sun (surface temps can hit 150°F), but a spray from the hose cools it instantly, and shaded turf stays comfortable.

Summer is actually the best time to think about this. You're living the reality of what your current landscape costs — in water, in time, in frustration. If you want to explore what a conversion would look like for your specific property, that's exactly the kind of conversation we have every week.

## The Short Version

Las Cruces summers are hard on landscapes and harder on water bills. The homeowners who get through July and August without problems are the ones who:

1. Water deep and infrequent instead of shallow and daily
2. Follow the watering schedule (and use a smart controller to automate it)
3. Cover bare soil — rock in the open, organic mulch in protected beds
4. Prep for monsoons before the first storm hits
5. Accept that some plants aren't meant for this climate — and stop forcing the issue
