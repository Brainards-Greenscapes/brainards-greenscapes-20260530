If you've ever watched your sprinklers running on a windy Las Cruces afternoon — spray drifting across the driveway, misting into the air, watering the block wall more than the plants — you already know the problem. A lot of that water never reaches the ground, let alone the roots.

Drip irrigation solves that. But it's not a universal answer, and making the wrong choice costs money either way. Here's an honest comparison from someone who installs both systems every week in the Mesilla Valley.

## The Efficiency Gap

The numbers aren't close. Drip irrigation operates at 90–95% efficiency — meaning almost every drop reaches the root zone. Sprinkler systems run at 50–75% efficiency, and in Las Cruces summer conditions (100°F+ air temp, 15–25 mph wind, single-digit humidity), the real-world number is often closer to 50%.

Where does the water go? Three places:

**Evaporation.** Spray heads throw water into the air as fine mist. On a July afternoon in Las Cruces, a significant percentage evaporates before it hits the ground. Drip emitters release water at soil level — evaporation is essentially zero.

**Wind drift.** Las Cruces averages 8–10 mph wind, with frequent gusts above 20 mph. That's enough to push spray patterns several feet off target. Drip lines sit on or under the ground — wind can't touch them.

**Runoff.** Spray heads apply water faster than compacted or caliche-heavy soil can absorb it. The excess runs off into the street. Drip applies water slowly — typically 1–4 gallons per hour per emitter — giving soil time to absorb it.

The practical effect: a drip zone serving the same plants uses 30–60% less water than a spray zone. On [DAMDWCA's tiered rates](/blog/xeriscaping-water-savings-las-cruces/#the-rate-breakdown) ($5.22–$6.99 per 1,000 gallons at higher tiers), that adds up fast.

## Where Sprinklers Still Make Sense

Drip can't do everything. If you have bermuda grass — the most common warm-season lawn in Las Cruces — you need [sprinkler heads](/services/irrigation/sprinkler-systems/) for even, broad coverage. Drip lines would leave stripes of green and brown across your yard.

**Sprinklers are the right choice for:**

- Bermuda grass lawns (need uniform coverage across the whole surface)
- Large turf areas where kids and pets play
- Any situation where you need broad, even water distribution

**Drip is the right choice for:**

- [Xeriscape beds](/services/landscaping/xeriscaping/) with desert-adapted plants spaced apart
- Tree and shrub zones (deep, targeted watering)
- Flower beds and garden areas
- Foundation plantings along the house
- Narrow strips between sidewalks and walls (spray heads overshoot these constantly)
- Any zone where wind is wrecking your spray patterns

The reality is that most well-designed Las Cruces irrigation systems use both — sprinklers on the grass, [drip](/services/irrigation/drip-irrigation/) on everything else.

## What a Conversion Actually Costs

If you already have a sprinkler system and want to convert some zones to drip, the cost is lower than most people expect.

**Converting an existing spray zone to drip:** $300–$1,200 per zone, depending on the area size and complexity. This includes removing the spray heads, adding a pressure regulator (drip runs at 20–30 PSI vs. 30–50 PSI for spray), installing a filter if you're on well water, and running drip tubing with emitters to each plant.

**For comparison, a new sprinkler zone from scratch:** $500–$1,000 per zone including pipe, heads, valve, and wiring.

**The payback math:** Say you're converting two landscape bed zones from spray to drip. Cost: roughly $600–$2,400. Water savings: 30–60% on those zones. If those two zones were using 3,000 gallons a month during summer, you're saving 900–1,800 gallons monthly. At LCU's higher tier ($3.73/1,000 gal), that's $3–$7 per month — modest. At DAMDWCA rates ($5.22+/1,000 gal), it's $5–$9 per month. Over a full watering season (March–October, roughly 8 months), you're saving $24–$72 per year.

The honest truth: the water savings alone take 3–8 years to pay back the conversion cost. But water savings aren't the only benefit — keep reading.

## Maintenance: The Real Cost Difference

This is where drip wins in ways that don't show up on the water bill.

**Sprinkler maintenance headaches in Las Cruces:**

- [Broken spray heads](/blog/irrigation-problems-las-cruces/#broken-or-stuck-spray-heads) from lawnmowers, foot traffic, and UV-brittled plastic — the single most common irrigation repair call
- Wind-blown coverage gaps that require constant nozzle adjustment
- Overspray onto sidewalks, driveways, and walls — a [watering restriction](/blog/summer-survival-guide-las-cruces-landscape/#the-watering-schedule-you-have-to-follow) violation waiting to happen
- Higher operating pressure means more stress on valves and fittings
- Runoff on compacted soil or slopes

**Drip maintenance in Las Cruces:**

- [Clogged emitters from hard water](/blog/irrigation-problems-las-cruces/#hard-water-and-clogged-emitters) — the main issue, solvable with periodic flushing and vinegar soaks
- UV damage to exposed tubing (bury it under 2–3 inches of rock and this goes away)
- [Monsoon runoff](/blog/monsoon-prep-las-cruces/) burying or shifting tubing (another reason to keep it covered)
- Occasional critter damage (rabbits and rodents will chew drip tubing, especially in the east mesa)

The difference in repair frequency is real. A sprinkler system in Las Cruces typically needs 2–4 head replacements per year and at least one coverage adjustment. A well-installed drip system needs flushing twice a year and the occasional emitter replacement. The parts are cheaper too — emitters cost under a dollar; spray heads run $3–$8.

## Wind: The Las Cruces Variable

This is the factor that tips the decision for a lot of properties. National irrigation guides don't weight wind heavily enough because most of the country doesn't deal with Las Cruces-level wind.

On a calm morning, a spray head's coverage pattern looks perfect — a nice even arc landing exactly where it should. By 2 p.m. on a typical June day, that same spray pattern is hitting your neighbor's driveway.

If you have zones where you're constantly adjusting spray arcs, replacing heads that get bent by wind load, or finding dry spots despite the system running correctly — those zones are candidates for drip conversion. The wind problem doesn't go away; you just have to take the water out of the air.

Rotary nozzles (like MP Rotators) are a middle-ground option — they throw heavier streams at lower angles that resist wind better than standard mist heads. They're worth trying before committing to a full drip conversion on lawn zones where you need broad coverage.

## The Hybrid Approach

The best irrigation systems we install in Las Cruces are hybrid: sprinklers where you need them, drip where it makes sense.

**A typical setup:**

- **Lawn zones:** Pop-up spray heads or rotary nozzles, 30–40 PSI, [programmed for deep watering](/blog/summer-survival-guide-las-cruces-landscape/#water-deep-not-often) 2–3 times per week
- **Landscape bed zones:** Drip tubing with pressure-compensating emitters at each plant, 20–25 PSI, running longer cycles fewer days
- **Tree zones:** Dedicated drip circuits with higher-flow emitters (2–4 GPH), deep soaking once a week in summer
- **Controller:** A single [smart controller](/services/irrigation/smart-controllers/) managing all zones with weather-based adjustments

This approach puts the right tool on the right job. You're not wasting spray water on a row of agaves, and you're not trying to water bermuda grass one drip emitter at a time.

## Making the Switch

If you're thinking about converting some zones, here's what to consider:

**Start with the worst spray zones.** Which zones give you the most trouble? The ones that overspray, the ones where heads keep breaking, the ones along the fence where wind carries the water away. Convert those first and see how it goes.

**Don't DIY the pressure regulator.** Drip runs at much lower pressure than spray. If you just swap heads for drip emitters without adding a pressure regulator, you'll blow out fittings and flood the zone. This is the one step that trips up most DIY conversions.

**Factor in your water source.** On city water (LCU, Moongate, DAMDWCA), you can skip inline filters — the [dissolved calcium that clogs emitters](/blog/irrigation-problems-las-cruces/#hard-water-and-clogged-emitters) passes through mesh filters anyway. On well water, add a filter at each drip valve.

**Get a [seasonal tune-up](/services/irrigation/seasonal-tune-ups/) after conversion.** We see a lot of DIY conversions where the zone run times weren't adjusted — drip needs to run longer than spray because it applies water more slowly. A quick audit catches this before plants start struggling.
